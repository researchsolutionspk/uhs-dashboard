Study at a Glance

A school-based endline survey evaluating the Empowering Girls programme across urban schools in Pakistan. The study measures changes in gender norms, safety perceptions, academic aspirations, and psychological wellbeing among Grade 8 students — with separate instruments for boys and girls using validated psychometric scales and Randomised Response Technique (RRT) for sensitive items.

Girls' Safety Behaviour Modifications

Frequency of behaviour adaptation to avoid safety risks (girls only, n=20)
Commute Safety Gap: Boys rate their commute to school as safer than girls (3.16 vs 2.85 on a 4-point scale). However, girls feel slightly safer in their school neighbourhood (3.10 vs 2.88 for boys), indicating that school areas may benefit from safety initiatives that carry forward into the commute route.
Behavioural Adaptation: Girls navigate safety concerns through adaptation — 80% sometimes or always change their clothing, 65% sometimes or always change their timing, and 65% sometimes or always change their route to school. These adaptations represent a significant daily burden and indicator of perceived risk.

Safety scale: 1=Not safe at all, 2=Not very safe, 3=Somewhat safe, 4=Very safe. Behaviour modification scale: 1=Always, 2=Sometimes, 3=Rarely, 4=Never. Travel times self-reported by students. Boys' school: School #141 (50 students enrolled Grade 7); Girls' school: 35–49 students enrolled.

Blame Attribution — Whose Fault?

Student responses to "touch_fault" — who is responsible when harassment occurs (1=Completely victim's fault, 5=Completely perpetrator's fault)
Witnessing Harassment: 70% of girls report having witnessed verbal harassment compared to only 44% of boys. This higher exposure among girls may reflect their greater vulnerability in public spaces and reinforces the importance of bystander intervention training as a programme component.
Blame Attribution: When asked who is responsible for harassment, boys show clearer attribution of fault to perpetrators — 48% strongly place blame on perpetrators. Girls show a more distributed response with 50% attributing it to "somewhat" the perpetrator (code 3), potentially reflecting internalised stigma that the programme aims to address.

Gender norms scale: 1=Strongly Disagree, 5=Strongly Agree. Items: G2="Girls should focus on household tasks"; G3="Education is equally important for boys and girls" (reverse-coded); G5="Children suffer when mothers work"; G6="Women need male protection to be safe". Blame scale: 1=Completely victim's fault through 5=Completely perpetrator's fault.

Wellbeing Scores: Both boys and girls report high wellbeing on the PQL scale, with family relationships receiving the highest scores (boys 4.48, girls 4.25). Girls report slightly higher friendship wellbeing (4.20 vs 3.92 for boys), while boys score higher on family and learning dimensions — suggesting complementary patterns of social connectedness.
Self-Efficacy Advantage: Girls score higher than boys on two of three self-efficacy items, particularly "I can think of solutions to problems" (2.80 vs 2.44) and "I can handle challenges" (2.45 vs 2.04). This aligns with girls' higher academic aspirations and suggests the programme may be building girls' internal agency.

PQL Scale: Items ask how often each applies in the past month (1=Never, 2=Almost Never, 3=Sometimes, 4=Often, 5=Almost Always). Self-Efficacy Scale: 3-point agreement scale (1=Disagree, 2=Neutral, 3=Agree). Higher scores indicate better outcomes on both scales.

Household Time Burden: Girls spend an average of 3.2 hours on caregiving and 3.0 hours on cleaning per day — a substantial non-academic time burden that may affect study time and school performance. Despite this burden, girls still outperform boys academically, suggesting significant resilience.
Harassment Reporting: 85% of girls (17/20) would report harassment to formal authorities, with family (65% — friends/siblings) being the primary informal channel. Only 5% would tell no one, suggesting a relatively supportive social network. The programme's emphasis on safe disclosure channels appears to be effective.
